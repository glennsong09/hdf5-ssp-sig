# HAZ-009: Thread-safe build uses a global lock

**HDF5 SSP vulnerability tags:** `LIB|OPS|TCD`

**Subsystem / component:** Thread-safe HDF5 builds, library-wide serialization behavior, language bindings, and workload scheduling under concurrent access.

**Hazard family:** H8 Operational, privacy, or supply-chain exposure

## Preconditions

- The deployment uses a thread-safe HDF5 build.
- Applications expect throughput gains from concurrent threads entering the library.
- Workloads are sensitive to latency spikes, backpressure, or missed service deadlines.

## Accident chain

### Trigger

Multiple threads contend on the library-wide lock in a thread-safe build.

### Unsafe state

The application appears concurrent but actual HDF5 execution is serialized, creating queue buildup and timing behavior far worse than operators expect.

### Loss / impact

Throughput collapses, latency spikes, time-sensitive pipelines miss deadlines, and operators may misdiagnose the issue as random performance instability.

## Risk assessment

Risk scoring used: **Severity (1-5) x Likelihood (1-5) = Risk (1-25)**

Risk bands: **1-5 Low, 6-10 Moderate, 11-15 High, 16-20 Very High, 21-25 Critical**

### Severity

**3 - Moderate.** The primary impact is availability and performance degradation rather than persistent corruption, but the operational consequences can still be serious.

### Likelihood

**4 - Likely.** The hazard is inherent to the build model and is readily triggered by multi-threaded workloads that assume scalable concurrency.

**Risk:** **12 (High)**

## Detectability

High once measured, but easy to misattribute. Profiling and contention telemetry reveal the lock, while casual observation may only show generic slowness or tail-latency spikes.

## Unsafe control actions

- **Not provided when needed:** The deployment does not document or benchmark the serialization behavior of the chosen thread-safe build.
- **Provided when unsafe:** Multi-threaded workloads are directed to a build whose concurrency model cannot meet their latency or throughput requirements.
- **Wrong timing/order:** Heavy HDF5 calls are scheduled concurrently in ways that amplify contention at critical pipeline stages.
- **Too long/too short:** Threads remain blocked on the global lock long enough to violate application timing assumptions or resource budgets.

## Safety constraints

- SC-1: Thread-safe build selection shall be paired with documented concurrency expectations and representative performance validation.
- SC-2: Workloads with strict latency or throughput requirements shall not rely on a serialized library path without explicit acceptance of that tradeoff.
- SC-3: Bindings and applications shall expose a supported threading model rather than implying unconstrained parallel entry to HDF5.

## Mitigations

### Prevention

Prefer multiprocessing or file sharding for heavy I/O, batch HDF5 operations to reduce call frequency, and benchmark representative workloads before rollout.

### Detection

Add contention profiling, latency monitoring, and CI benchmarks that compare thread-safe builds against expected workload behavior.

### Recovery / containment

Throttle concurrent callers, reconfigure workloads toward process-level parallelism, and move latency-sensitive paths off the serialized build when contention becomes operationally unsafe.

## Tests / evidence

- Crash injection: Verify that stalled or blocked threads during abrupt termination do not leave downstream services assuming completed HDF5 work.
- Malformed-input or fuzz coverage: Exercise error-heavy threaded paths to ensure lock contention does not hide or amplify failure behavior.
- Concurrency coverage: Run stress tests with realistic thread counts, mixed bindings, and representative I/O patterns to quantify serialization impact.
- Runtime validation / telemetry: Track lock contention, queue depth, request latency, and throughput under threaded load.
