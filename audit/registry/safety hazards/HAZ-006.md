# HAZ-006: False sense of durability from flush

**HDF5 SSP vulnerability tags:** `OPS|LIB`

**Subsystem / component:** `H5Fflush`, related flush APIs, metadata cache eviction, and the handoff from library buffers to OS and storage durability.

**Hazard family:** H3 Unsafe durability signaling, H2 Hybrid divergence

## Preconditions

- Applications rely on `flush` as a checkpoint or durability boundary.
- The OS, filesystem, or storage stack may buffer writes beyond what HDF5 directly controls.
- Operational guidance does not clearly define what a successful flush means in the target environment.

## Accident chain

### Trigger

The library reports flush success, but lower layers have not yet made the required bytes durably safe.

### Unsafe state

The application believes the file is durable while disk, memory, and storage-layer state still diverge.

### Loss / impact

Checkpoint assumptions become false, recent work can be lost after a crash, and users may act on an unsafe belief that data has been committed.

## Risk assessment

Risk scoring used: **Severity (1-5) x Likelihood (1-5) = Risk (1-25)**

Risk bands: **1-5 Low, 6-10 Moderate, 11-15 High, 16-20 Very High, 21-25 Critical**

### Severity

**4 - Major.** Incorrect durability signaling can invalidate checkpoints and cause silent loss of recent work after interruption.

### Likelihood

**3 - Possible.** The hazard depends on deployment semantics, but flush misunderstandings are common across local, network, and object-backed environments.

**Risk:** **12 (High)**

## Detectability

Low to moderate. The mismatch often appears only after a crash or power event, unless reopen tests and platform-specific durability checks are already in place.

## Unsafe control actions

- **Not provided when needed:** The system does not define or verify the durability boundary required by the application before treating flush success as meaningful.
- **Provided when unsafe:** Flush success is used as a "safe to proceed" or "safe to publish" signal even when lower layers may still be buffering writes.
- **Wrong timing/order:** Metadata, application state, or downstream notifications are advanced before the environment has provided the intended durability guarantee.
- **Too long/too short:** Flush is treated as sufficient for long-term persistence when the operation only covers a shorter or weaker buffering boundary.

## Safety constraints

- SC-1: The system shall document what `flush` guarantees and does not guarantee for each supported deployment environment.
- SC-2: Applications shall not treat flush success as a durable checkpoint unless the required lower-layer persistence guarantees are part of the design.
- SC-3: High-value checkpoints shall use an operational durability strategy that matches the real storage semantics.

## Mitigations

### Prevention

Combine `H5Fflush` with close, snapshot, copy, or generation-switch strategies where stronger durability is needed, and document environment-specific durability expectations.

### Detection

Add reopen-after-crash tests, platform-specific durability validation, and telemetry that distinguishes flush success from stronger checkpoint completion.

### Recovery / containment

Recover from the last verified durable checkpoint, not merely the last successful flush, and isolate workflows that depend on stronger persistence guarantees than the environment actually provides.

## Tests / evidence

- Crash injection: Trigger crashes immediately after flush returns and verify which bytes are actually durable after reopen.
- Malformed-input or fuzz coverage: Exercise reopen logic with files captured after flush but before lower-layer persistence completed.
- Concurrency coverage: Verify that readers or downstream publishers do not treat flush completion as a stronger guarantee than the deployment supports.
- Runtime validation / telemetry: Record flush calls, checkpoint publication events, reopen outcomes, and any mismatch between expected and observed durability.
