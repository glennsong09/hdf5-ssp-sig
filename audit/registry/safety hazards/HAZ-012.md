# HAZ-012: Recovery tooling limits

**HDF5 SSP vulnerability tags:** `OPS|LIB|FMT`

**Subsystem / component:** Recovery workflows, corruption handling, `h5clear`, integrity validation, and operational backup or replay strategies.

**Hazard family:** H1 On-disk metadata inconsistency, H2 Hybrid divergence, H8 Operational, privacy, or supply-chain exposure

## Preconditions

- A file has entered a corrupted, incomplete, or suspect state.
- Operators expect recovery tools to restore access or salvage data.
- The available tooling only covers a subset of failure modes, such as certain SWMR-created cases.

## Accident chain

### Trigger

A file requires recovery after corruption or incomplete persistence, but the available tooling cannot repair the specific damage pattern.

### Unsafe state

The workflow depends on a recovery path that does not actually exist for the observed metadata or on-disk inconsistency.

### Loss / impact

Recovery efforts stall, data remains inaccessible, and operators lose time or confidence while the chance of safe restoration decreases.

## Risk assessment

Risk scoring used: **Severity (1-5) x Likelihood (1-5) = Risk (1-25)**

Risk bands: **1-5 Low, 6-10 Moderate, 11-15 High, 16-20 Very High, 21-25 Critical**

### Severity

**4 - Major.** Limited recovery tooling can turn a contained file-safety incident into a durable data-loss or service-availability problem.

### Likelihood

**3 - Possible.** Corruption is not routine, but when it happens the mismatch between expected and actual recovery capability is a realistic operational risk.

**Risk:** **12 (High)**

## Detectability

High once recovery begins. The problem becomes obvious when tools fail to repair the file, but that is often too late to avoid escalation.

## Unsafe control actions

- **Not provided when needed:** The system does not maintain last-good generations, replayable inputs, or backups for failure modes beyond tool-assisted repair.
- **Provided when unsafe:** Operators rely on recovery tooling as a primary safety strategy even when its coverage is explicitly limited.
- **Wrong timing/order:** Corruption is discovered late because integrity validation happens only after recovery is needed.
- **Too long/too short:** Incident response spends too long attempting unsupported repair paths before switching to fallback recovery plans.

## Safety constraints

- SC-1: Recovery planning shall assume that tooling support is incomplete for many metadata-corruption scenarios.
- SC-2: Pipelines shall preserve a last-known-good generation, backup, or replay path for data that matters operationally.
- SC-3: Integrity validation shall run early enough to detect corruption before unsupported recovery becomes the only option.

## Mitigations

### Prevention

Treat recovery as exceptional, keep last-good generations and backups, preserve replayable input streams, and design pipelines to tolerate file replacement rather than in-place rescue.

### Detection

Automate integrity validation in pipelines, alert on early signs of corruption, and document which failure classes available tools can and cannot address.

### Recovery / containment

Escalate quickly to backups or replay when tooling limits are reached, preserve suspect files for forensic analysis, and avoid repeated write attempts against corrupted artifacts.

## Tests / evidence

- Crash injection: Generate known corruption patterns and verify when recovery tools help, when they do not, and how fallback procedures behave.
- Malformed-input or fuzz coverage: Exercise recovery and validation tooling with files containing unsupported metadata damage patterns.
- Concurrency coverage: Verify that recovery workflows and integrity checks behave safely when readers or operators encounter suspect files at the same time.
- Runtime validation / telemetry: Track corruption detections, repair-tool outcomes, fallback-to-backup events, and recovery time to resolution.
