# HAZ-011: Deleting data does not shrink files by default

**HDF5 SSP vulnerability tags:** `OPS|LIB|FMT`

**Subsystem / component:** File-space management, free-space reuse, delete-in-place workflows, and storage-capacity monitoring.

**Hazard family:** H4 Free-space aliasing or reuse, H8 Operational, privacy, or supply-chain exposure

## Preconditions

- Workflows delete or overwrite data inside existing files.
- Operators assume deleting content will reduce physical file size promptly.
- Capacity planning and monitoring focus on logical data volume rather than physical on-disk growth.

## Accident chain

### Trigger

Data is deleted or replaced in-place, but the file retains its physical size and accumulates internal free space and fragmentation.

### Unsafe state

The file's logical contents shrink while physical storage usage and fragmentation continue to grow in ways operators do not expect.

### Loss / impact

Storage bloat, fragmentation, and capacity incidents occur, and delete-heavy workflows become operationally unsafe or inefficient.

## Risk assessment

Risk scoring used: **Severity (1-5) x Likelihood (1-5) = Risk (1-25)**

Risk bands: **1-5 Low, 6-10 Moderate, 11-15 High, 16-20 Very High, 21-25 Critical**

### Severity

**3 - Moderate.** The issue usually affects storage efficiency and operations rather than immediate correctness, but it can still trigger space-related failures and long-term instability.

### Likelihood

**4 - Likely.** Delete-in-place expectations are common, and the mismatch between logical deletion and physical size is easy to overlook until bloat is substantial.

**Risk:** **12 (High)**

## Detectability

Moderate. Physical-size monitoring can reveal the drift, but teams often notice only after storage pressure or fragmentation has already become costly.

## Unsafe control actions

- **Not provided when needed:** The system does not communicate that deletion frees internal space without shrinking the physical file.
- **Provided when unsafe:** Delete-heavy workflows continue using a file layout that is no longer operationally efficient or capacity-safe.
- **Wrong timing/order:** Files are repeatedly mutated in place when rolling, partitioned, or repacked publication patterns would be safer.
- **Too long/too short:** Compaction or repack operations are delayed too long, allowing fragmentation and bloat to accumulate past safe operating limits.

## Safety constraints

- SC-1: Capacity planning shall distinguish logical data deletion from physical file-size reduction.
- SC-2: Delete-heavy workflows shall define a compaction, repack, or rolling-file strategy before production use.
- SC-3: Monitoring shall track physical-versus-logical size drift and alert when bloat exceeds operational thresholds.

## Mitigations

### Prevention

Adopt file-space management strategies, schedule repack or compact jobs, and prefer rolling or time-partitioned files over frequent delete-in-place mutation.

### Detection

Monitor logical versus physical size drift, track fragmentation indicators, and review delete-heavy schemas for storage bloat trends.

### Recovery / containment

Repack bloated files, rotate to new generations, and isolate delete-heavy workloads from environments with tight storage headroom until compaction is complete.

## Tests / evidence

- Crash injection: Verify that repack or compaction workflows fail safely if interrupted and do not worsen fragmentation or lose reachable data.
- Malformed-input or fuzz coverage: Exercise file-space metadata validation on files with heavy delete and reuse histories.
- Concurrency coverage: Test repack, deletion, and reader workflows together to ensure compaction planning does not introduce new access hazards.
- Runtime validation / telemetry: Track file size growth, internal free-space ratios, repack frequency, and alerts on storage bloat thresholds.
